Report on the Alphageeks meeting

I went to a meeting of an Israeli computers group called "Alphageeks" today and would like to write a report about it when my memory is still fresh.

I was a little tardy this time, because I got prepared late and left home even later, but the first presentation still did not start when I got there a few minutes after the schedule. It took me a lot of time to find the building, because I did not print a good map in advance and instead relied on my memory of the general location of the place and people's directions.

One thing I noticed was that the location left a lot to be desired. The building is old, the stairs suffered from a lot of decay, and the elevator did not seem to work, nor could really be trusted (as someone I left with said later on). I ended up at a studio apartment on the third floor without too many chairs, with a fridge and a counter with a lot of food and drink (including many beer bottles - I don't drink but it seemed like a nice bonus), and with many people there. Since I didn't have a chair, I opted to stand during the two talks that I stayed to watch.

The first thing we did was a round of introductions

In any case, I placed the bottle of juice that I had bought on the way on the counter, took a cup from a different bottle of juice (Lemonade - yum.) and went to stand and watch the talk about HTML 5. While it was as a general rule, not a very technical talk, it was pretty cool, explaining the history of HTML 5, some of the new features that we can expect from it, and a lot of promise. We discussed the fact that Internet Explorer is still lagging behind other browsers in its support for even older standards, and I was disappointed to find out that MSIE 9 still does not support .xhtml or the application/xml content-type. We also discussed CSS 3, and the presenter said that CSS 2.1 was already good enough, and that we could live without gradients or rounded corners.

He mentioned that his web-design process works by getting a page style as an image from his graphics' designer, sending it somewhere ("to India, etc.") and getting a 100% squeaky clean and semantic XHTML design, and then using jQuery to communicate with the JSON API, and stuff like that.

Then there was a break, where I ended up discussing my grand unified vision for a typesetting system I'm writing as an alternative to TeX and LaTeX, and also asked a little about XSL-FO and Apache FOP, and we concluded that good internationalisation may be a problem with some of the XSL-FO implementations. The Apache FOP people told me they would rather not implement Bi-directionality (e.g: mixed Arabic/Hebrew/etc. and Latin) until version 1.0 and the last release was 0.95 since 13 months ago.
